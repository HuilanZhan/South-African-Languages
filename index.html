What you speak decides who you are?


A language and identity struggle in post-apartheid South Africa



Huilan Zhan


On a flight from Cape Town to Johannesburg May 30, the flight attendant broadcasted that they would offer services in three languages, English, Afrikaans and Xhosa.


Xhosa is the dominant native language in Eastern Cape province in South Africa. Nelson Mandela firstly had his forename in Xhosa, “Rolihlahla”, which meant “troublemaker.” Mandela got the English name “Nelson” by his teacher in a local Methodist school.


Having multiple language identities is a common thing for South African society. The country has 11 official languages: Zulu, Xhosa, Afrikaans, English, Northern Sotho, Tswana, Sotho, Tsonga, Swazi, Venda and Ndebele. National and each provincial government use at least two of them.


According to The South African National Census of 2011, Zulu is the most common language as a first language in South Africa, making up 22.7 percent, followed by Xhosa, Afrikaans and English.


Except Afrikaans and English, other nine languages are Bantu languages, which originate from Central Africa and were expanded southward three thousand years ago. Under apartheid, the nine languages were not recognized as official languages.


In 1996, the “Constitution of the Republic of South Africa” specifies that a Pan South African Language Board established by national legislation should promote the development and use of the eleven newly recognized official languages.


However, the legal status of all languages cannot guarantee economic equality of them. After apartheid, the socio-economic privileges of Afrikaans and English still cast a shadow over native language speakers.

A linguistic path


“English is the passport to happiness,” said Harold Herman, a professor at University of Western Cape.


Western Cape and Gauteng are the top two provinces in terms of the percentage of higher education population in provincial population, according to the 2011 census. They also have the highest percentages of English speakers in provincial population in 2011.


Sibusiso Maneli, a junior student in public administration and economy at University of Western Cape, said English meant a lot to him. He said, “English is more dominant at universities and companies. it’s a language of academic research and business communication.”


Maneli comes from Eastern Cape. He natively speaks Xhosa and Zulu and has been learning English and Afrikaans from grade one in primary school.


Eleanor du Plooy from Institute for Justice and Reconciliation, a non-government organization to promote justice after apartheid, said that Afrikaans speakers experienced a higher employment rate than other South African language groups.


According to the 2011 census, Western Cape and Gauteng had a relatively high economic growth rate in 2011, compared to other provinces. They had bigger provincial contribution to South African economy.

An imbalance of information


Media presentation in different languages is imbalanced in South Africa.


“English may be a filter,” said Asonda Ngoasheng, a journalist and a professor at Cape Peninsula University of Technology.


She said the elite media may not reach the population in developing communities.


South African media such as South African Broadcasting Corporation preferably use English as their language in the post-apartheid era, according to Jon Orman, the author of the book “Language Policy and National-Building in Post-Apartheid South Africa.” Orman found that Afrikaans programs ran English subtitles, but it was hard to see English programs with subtitles in Afrikaans and African indigenous languages.


Native languages also face challenges in education. Maneli said, “We don’t speak our indigenous languages often. It has lost relevance in academic space.”


In Maneli’s class, English works as the language for exams and questions. “It has an impact of how we internalize knowledge.”


In his views, the language issue becomes hindrance in students’ progress. Students who come from rural areas and haven’t been taught in English have difficulties in understanding and contextualizing this language.

A question of identity


The language landscape in South Africa poses a question of identity for its people and society.


“Social cohesion, identity recognition, they are connected with languages.” Maneli said.


The 1996 Constitution says the state must take measures to advance the use of indigenous languages. It also says governments may use particular official languages for their purposes based on provincial situation.


Maneli thinks there is a lack of plans about how we implement such policies. “How do we coordinate these languages into the mainstream, economic and academic development?”


Herman is concerned that the whites would decide what’s important in knowledge. “There is not enough literature and scholarships in other languages,” he said.


Maneli added, “Even some language policies in response to the issue in the higher education system could attempt to democratize, and to bring back the dignity to the indigenous people.”


“If you talk to a man in a language he understands, that goes to his head. If you talk to him in his language, that goes to his heart,” said Nelson Mandela.
